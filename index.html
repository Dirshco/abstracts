Project 1: Telemedicine in Nursing Homes / Post-Acute Care

Systematic Review Digital Health Health Services

Proposed Title

Effectiveness of Telemedicine in Nursing Homes and Post-Acute Care: A Systematic Review of Clinical Outcomes and Healthcare Utilisation

Abstract Draft (Editable)

Background: Residents in nursing homes and post-acute care facilities have high rates of emergency department transfers and hospital admissions. Telemedicine has expanded rapidly, but its clinical effectiveness in long-term care remains unclear. This systematic review aimed to evaluate the impact of telemedicine interventions on acute care utilisation and patient outcomes in nursing home populations. Methods: A systematic review was conducted following PRISMA guidance. PubMed/MEDLINE, Embase, CINAHL, and Cochrane Library were searched from inception to [Month Year]. Eligible studies evaluated telemedicine interventions in nursing homes, skilled nursing facilities, or long-term care settings. Outcomes included hospital admissions, ED transfers, mortality, patient satisfaction, staff satisfaction, and cost. Study quality was assessed using Newcastle-Ottawa Scale or Cochrane risk-of-bias tools. Results: XX studies including approximately XX,XXX residents were included. Telemedicine models included video consultations, tele-geriatric programs, remote monitoring, and urgent tele-triage. Most studies reported reductions in ED transfers and hospital admissions, particularly when integrated with escalation protocols. Evidence for mortality benefit was inconsistent. Several studies reported high satisfaction among staff and families and potential cost savings. Implementation barriers included staff training needs, connectivity issues, and inconsistent clinician engagement. Conclusion: Telemedicine may reduce acute care utilisation and improve access to timely clinical decision-making in nursing homes. Future research should focus on standardised outcome reporting, cost-effectiveness analysis, and patient-centred outcomes to guide scalable implementation.

How to Make This Abstract Stronger (High-Scoring Strategy)

  • Add a quantitative outcome: e.g. "pooled reduction in ED transfers" (if meta-analysis possible).
  • Focus on "avoidable transfers" and sustainability impact (high relevance for UK NHS).
  • Include barriers + facilitators (implementation science makes it more publishable).
  • Highlight workforce shortages and rural access issues.

PubMed Search Strategy (Example)

("nursing homes"[MeSH] OR "skilled nursing facility" OR "long-term care" OR "care home" OR "post-acute care") AND ("telemedicine"[MeSH] OR telehealth OR "virtual care" OR "remote consultation" OR "video visit") AND (hospitalization OR "emergency department" OR readmission OR mortality OR outcomes)

Data Extraction Table (Suggested Columns)

  • Author / Year
  • Country
  • Study Design
  • Population size
  • Telemedicine model (video consult / remote monitoring / tele-triage)
  • Comparator (usual care)
  • Outcomes measured (ED transfer, hospital admission, mortality, cost)
  • Main results
  • Limitations
  • Risk of bias score

Project 2: AI Clinical Documentation Tools and Patient Safety

Scoping Review Innovation Patient Safety

Proposed Title

Artificial Intelligence Clinical Documentation Tools in Healthcare: A Scoping Review of Efficiency Gains, Patient Safety Risks, and Implementation Barriers

Abstract Draft (Editable)

Background: AI-driven clinical documentation tools, including ambient scribes and automated note-generation systems, are increasingly used to reduce administrative burden. While early evidence suggests improved workflow efficiency, concerns remain regarding patient safety, documentation accuracy, bias, privacy, and medico-legal accountability. This scoping review aimed to summarise current evidence on AI documentation tools in clinical practice. Methods: A scoping review was conducted following PRISMA-ScR guidance. PubMed/MEDLINE, Embase, Scopus, and Cochrane Library were searched from inception to [Month Year]. Studies were included if they evaluated AI-based documentation tools used in clinical workflows (real-world or simulated). Data extracted included clinical setting, tool type, outcomes (time saved, note quality, error rates), patient safety concerns, clinician satisfaction, and implementation barriers. Results: XX studies were included. Most were observational pilot studies conducted in outpatient and emergency department settings. Reported benefits included reduced documentation time and improved clinician satisfaction. Patient safety concerns included inaccurate transcription, omission of key details, inappropriate insertion of information, and unclear accountability for errors. Data privacy, patient consent, and EMR integration challenges were frequently reported. Evidence quality was limited by small sample sizes and short follow-up periods. Conclusion: AI documentation tools may improve efficiency but raise important safety and governance concerns. Robust prospective studies with standardised safety outcomes (hallucination rates, omission errors, downstream harm) are needed before widespread adoption.

How to Make This Abstract Stronger

  • Include the concept of "hallucination" and define it in medical documentation context.
  • Add "clinical governance frameworks" and "auditability" (important for NHS/UK).
  • Group results by setting: ED vs outpatient vs inpatient.
  • Include ethical angle: consent + privacy + medicolegal responsibility.

PubMed Search Strategy (Example)

("artificial intelligence"[MeSH] OR AI OR "large language model" OR LLM OR "ambient scribe" OR "digital scribe") AND ("clinical documentation" OR "medical note" OR "progress note" OR "electronic health record") AND (patient safety OR accuracy OR errors OR quality OR bias OR privacy)

Data Extraction Table (Suggested Columns)

  • Author / Year
  • Tool name / technology type
  • Setting (ED, outpatient, inpatient)
  • Study design
  • Primary outcome (time saved, burnout score)
  • Error types (omission, insertion, misinterpretation)
  • Safety reporting method
  • Implementation barriers
  • Regulatory/consent issues mentioned

Project 3: Hyperkalemia in Obstructive Uropathy-Associated AKI

Systematic Review Clinical Research Nephrology

Proposed Title

Hyperkalemia in Acute Kidney Injury Secondary to Obstructive Uropathy: A Systematic Review of Predictors, Management Strategies, and Outcomes

Abstract Draft (Editable)

Background: Obstructive uropathy is a common cause of acute kidney injury (AKI) and may result in life-threatening hyperkalemia. Although relief of obstruction is the definitive management, predictors and outcomes of hyperkalemia in this setting remain poorly characterised. This systematic review aimed to evaluate predictors of hyperkalemia and associated clinical outcomes in patients with obstructive uropathy-associated AKI. Methods: A systematic review was conducted according to PRISMA guidance. PubMed/MEDLINE, Embase, and Cochrane Library were searched from inception to [Month Year]. Studies reporting adult patients with obstructive uropathy-related AKI and hyperkalemia incidence or outcomes were included. Two reviewers extracted data and assessed study quality using Newcastle-Ottawa Scale. Results: XX studies including X,XXX patients were included. Hyperkalemia incidence varied widely. Predictors included bilateral hydronephrosis, oliguria/anuria, prolonged obstruction, and baseline chronic kidney disease. Severe hyperkalemia was associated with increased ICU admission and dialysis requirement. Relief of obstruction through catheterisation or nephrostomy was associated with improved potassium and renal recovery, though post-obstructive diuresis and electrolyte instability were common. Conclusion: Hyperkalemia is a clinically significant complication of obstructive AKI and is associated with higher rates of ICU admission and dialysis. Early recognition and urgent decompression may improve outcomes. Prospective studies are needed to standardise risk stratification and management pathways.

How to Make This Abstract Stronger

  • Add subgroup analysis: malignancy-related obstruction vs BPH vs stones.
  • Add the clinical urgency angle: arrhythmia risk and ICU escalation.
  • Include outcomes of interest: renal recovery time, dialysis-free survival.
  • Emphasize relevance to acute medicine and emergency care.

PubMed Search Strategy (Example)

("acute kidney injury"[MeSH] OR AKI OR "acute renal failure") AND (obstruction OR "obstructive uropathy" OR hydronephrosis OR "urinary tract obstruction") AND (hyperkalemia OR potassium OR "electrolyte imbalance") AND (nephrostomy OR catheterization OR decompression OR dialysis OR outcomes)

Data Extraction Table (Suggested Columns)

  • Author / Year
  • Cause of obstruction (BPH, malignancy, stone)
  • Bilateral vs unilateral
  • Potassium levels (mean, peak)
  • Dialysis requirement
  • Mortality / ICU admission
  • Time to decompression
  • Renal recovery outcomes

Project 4: Reducing Readmissions from Skilled Nursing Facilities

Systematic Review Quality Improvement Sustainability

Proposed Title

Interventions to Reduce 30-Day Hospital Readmissions from Skilled Nursing Facilities: A Systematic Review of Transitional Care and Quality Improvement Models

Abstract Draft (Editable)

Background: Hospital readmissions from skilled nursing facilities (SNFs) and post-acute care settings contribute significantly to healthcare costs and patient morbidity. Many readmissions are potentially preventable through improved transitional care, medication reconciliation, and early clinical reassessment. This systematic review evaluated interventions aimed at reducing hospital readmissions from SNFs and nursing homes. Methods: A systematic review was conducted according to PRISMA guidance. PubMed/MEDLINE, Embase, CINAHL, and Cochrane Library were searched from inception to [Month Year]. Studies were included if they evaluated interventions in SNFs or long-term care settings and reported outcomes including 30-day readmission rates, ED transfers, mortality, or cost. Study quality was assessed using Cochrane risk-of-bias tools or Newcastle-Ottawa Scale. Due to heterogeneity, results were synthesised narratively. Results: XX studies including XX,XXX residents were included. Interventions included transitional care programs, structured hospital-to-SNF communication tools, pharmacist-led medication reconciliation, early warning nursing protocols, and telemedicine physician support. Most studies reported reductions in readmission rates, especially when interventions combined medication review with early follow-up. Barriers included staffing limitations and inconsistent protocol adoption. Conclusion: Multidisciplinary transitional care interventions may reduce readmissions from SNFs. Workforce capacity and structured communication systems appear critical for success. Further high-quality studies are needed to identify scalable and cost-effective strategies.

How to Make This Abstract Stronger

  • Include cost analysis and "bed capacity" (high NHS relevance).
  • Group interventions into categories and compare which seems strongest.
  • Highlight avoidable readmissions as a patient safety issue.
  • Add implementation barriers as a formal theme.

PubMed Search Strategy (Example)

("skilled nursing facility" OR "nursing home" OR "post-acute care" OR "long-term care") AND (readmission OR rehospitalization OR "hospital transfer" OR "emergency department") AND (intervention OR program OR "transitional care" OR telemedicine OR pharmacist OR "medication reconciliation")

Data Extraction Table (Suggested Columns)

  • Author / Year
  • Intervention type
  • Duration of program
  • Staff involved (MD/NP/pharmacist/nurse)
  • Baseline readmission rate
  • Post-intervention readmission rate
  • Cost outcomes
  • Implementation issues

Project 5: Teaching Clinical Reasoning to International Medical Graduates

Scoping Review Education Workforce Development

Proposed Title

Educational Strategies for Teaching Clinical Reasoning to International Medical Graduates: A Scoping Review

Abstract Draft (Editable)

Background: International medical graduates (IMGs) represent a critical component of the healthcare workforce in both the UK and US. Despite clinical competence, many IMGs face challenges adapting to local healthcare systems, communication standards, and clinical reasoning expectations. This scoping review aimed to identify educational strategies used to teach clinical reasoning and professional integration skills to IMGs and internationally trained physicians. Methods: A scoping review was conducted following PRISMA-ScR guidance. PubMed/MEDLINE, ERIC, Scopus, and Embase were searched from inception to [Month Year]. Studies were included if they evaluated educational interventions for IMGs or internationally trained physicians targeting clinical reasoning, diagnostic accuracy, communication, or professionalism. Data were extracted on intervention type, duration, outcomes, and assessment methods. Results: XX studies were included. Interventions included case-based learning, simulation training, structured mentorship programs, OSCE coaching, communication workshops, and e-learning modules. Many studies reported improved exam performance and increased confidence, though objective clinical reasoning assessment methods varied. Successful programs commonly included longitudinal mentorship and feedback-based learning. Barriers included time constraints, faculty availability, and inconsistent standardisation of competency assessment. Conclusion: Educational interventions for IMGs appear beneficial, particularly those incorporating mentorship and structured simulation-based training. More rigorous studies using validated clinical reasoning and patient safety outcomes are needed to guide scalable program design for workforce integration.

How to Make This Abstract Stronger

  • Add workforce framing: IMGs reduce staffing gaps in NHS/US healthcare.
  • Include measurable outcomes: OSCE scores, board pass rates, diagnostic accuracy.
  • Highlight mentorship as a recurring success factor.
  • Add professionalism + communication as patient safety issues.

PubMed Search Strategy (Example)

("international medical graduates" OR IMG OR "internationally trained physicians") AND ("clinical reasoning" OR diagnosis OR "medical education" OR simulation OR OSCE OR mentorship) AND (outcomes OR performance OR competency OR "professionalism")

Data Extraction Table (Suggested Columns)

  • Author / Year
  • Country (UK/US/Canada/Australia)
  • Type of IMG program
  • Teaching method (simulation, OSCE, case-based)
  • Duration (weeks/months)
  • Outcomes measured (confidence, exam pass, OSCE score)
  • Validated tools used? (Yes/No)
  • Strengths / limitations
